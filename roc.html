Why the ROC matters

The Z-transform is an infinite sum. Like any infinite series, it converges for some values of z and diverges for others. The set of z values where X(z) converges is called the Region of Convergence (ROC). Without specifying the ROC, the Z-transform is ambiguous — the same algebraic expression X(z) can correspond to completely different time-domain signals.

Shape of the ROC

Always an annulus (ring)

The ROC of any Z-transform is always a ring-shaped region in the z-plane:

R₁ < |z| < R₂

This is because convergence depends only on |z|, not on the angle of z. If X(z) converges at some z₀, it converges at every point with the same magnitude. The boundaries R₁ and R₂ are determined by the pole locations of X(z).

Special cases:

  • Right-sided (causal) signals: ROC is the exterior of a circle: |z| > R₁ (R₂ = ∞)
  • Left-sided (anti-causal) signals: ROC is the interior of a circle: |z| < R₂ (R₁ = 0)
  • Two-sided signals: ROC is a proper ring: R₁ < |z| < R₂
  • Finite-duration signals: ROC is the entire z-plane (possibly excluding z = 0 or z = ∞)
The ROC is like the safe operating range of a pressure vessel. The vessel (the sum) can handle pressures (|z| values) in a specific band — too high and it bursts outward (the positive-n terms diverge), too low and it collapses inward (the negative-n terms diverge). The safe range is always a ring between two pressure limits.

ROC Rules

The seven rules you need

  1. The ROC is a ring centred on the origin: R₁ < |z| < R₂
  2. The ROC contains no poles. By definition — X(z) is infinite at a pole, so it can't converge there.
  3. Finite-duration sequences: ROC is the entire z-plane, except possibly z = 0 and/or z = ∞
  4. Right-sided sequences: ROC extends outward from the outermost pole: |z| > max pole
  5. Left-sided sequences: ROC extends inward from the innermost pole: |z| < min pole
  6. Two-sided sequences: ROC is a ring between consecutive pole radii
  7. The ROC is a connected region — never a union of disjoint rings

ROC and Causality

The exterior means causal

A right-sided (causal) sequence has x[n] = 0 for n < 0. Its Z-transform sum has only non-negative powers of z⁻¹, which converge for large |z|. Therefore:

  • Causal → ROC is the exterior of a circle containing the outermost pole
  • Anti-causal → ROC is the interior of a circle inside the innermost pole
  • Stable + causal → ROC is the exterior of a circle AND includes the unit circle → all poles inside unit circle
A system is both causal AND stable if and only if ALL poles lie strictly inside the unit circle.

The Ambiguity Example

Same X(z), three different signals

Consider:

X(z) = z² / [(z − ½)(z − 2)]

Poles at z = ½ and z = 2. There are three possible ROCs:

  1. |z| > 2: Both terms are causal → x[n] = [2ⁿ − (½)ⁿ] · 2 · u[n]. Right-sided, unstable.
  2. ½ < |z| < 2: The ½-pole term is causal, the 2-pole term is anti-causal → a two-sided signal. Stable (ROC includes unit circle).
  3. |z| < ½: Both terms are anti-causal → x[n] = −[2ⁿ − (½)ⁿ] · 2 · u[−n−1]. Left-sided, unstable.

Three completely different time-domain sequences — same X(z) formula. The ROC is what disambiguates.

This ambiguity is like reading the word "bank" without context. It could mean a riverbank, a financial institution, or a billiard shot. The ROC is the context that resolves the ambiguity — it tells you which meaning (which signal) the speaker (the transform) intends.

ROC and the Unit Circle

The DTFT exists when the ROC includes the unit circle

The DTFT of x[n] is obtained by evaluating X(z) on the unit circle (z = ejω). This evaluation is only valid if the unit circle lies within the ROC. Therefore:

  • Stable signals have a DTFT (unit circle in ROC)
  • Unstable signals do NOT have a DTFT — their Z-transform exists, but you can't evaluate it on the unit circle because the sum diverges there

This is one reason the Z-transform is more general than the DTFT: it can analyse unstable systems that the DTFT cannot even represent.

Computing the ROC

From poles and signal type

Given a rational X(z) = N(z)/D(z):

  1. Find all poles (roots of D(z))
  2. Order poles by magnitude: |p₁| ≤ |p₂| ≤ ... ≤ |pₙ|
  3. The possible ROCs are the rings between consecutive pole magnitudes, plus the exterior and interior
  4. Choose based on the signal type:
    • If causal: pick the exterior |z| > |pₙ|
    • If anti-causal: pick the interior |z| < |p₁|
    • If two-sided: pick a ring between some |pᵢ| < |z| < |pᵢ₊₁|

ROC Under Operations

How properties affect convergence

  • Addition: ROC(X+Y) ⊇ ROC(X) ∩ ROC(Y) — might be larger due to cancellation
  • Multiplication (convolution): ROC(X·Y) ⊇ ROC(X) ∩ ROC(Y)
  • Scaling by aⁿ: ROC scales by |a| — all boundaries multiply by |a|
  • Time reversal: ROC inverts — R₁ < |z| < R₂ becomes 1/R₂ < |z| < 1/R₁

The ROC as a window into signal "energy direction"

The ROC encodes which direction in time the signal's energy is concentrated. An outward ROC (|z| > R) means the signal's energy moves forward in time — it's causal. An inward ROC (|z| < R) means energy moves backward — anti-causal. A ring means energy is spread in both directions but bounded. In this view, the ROC is not just a convergence technicality — it's a physical statement about where the signal "lives" in time and how fast it grows or decays.

This "energy direction" interpretation is the author's pedagogical framing. While consistent with the mathematics, it's not standard terminology in DSP literature and should not be cited as a formal definition.
