Inverse Z-Transform

Three methods to recover x[n] from X(z) — partial fractions, power series, and contour integration

3 Methods
PFE Most practical
∮ Contour integral

The Formal Inverse

x[n] = (1/2πj) ∮C X(z) · zn−1 dz

The contour integral definition

The formal inverse Z-transform is a contour integral around a closed path C within the ROC. While mathematically elegant, we rarely compute this directly. Instead, we use three practical methods that avoid explicit contour integration in most cases.

Method 1: Partial Fraction Expansion

The workhorse method

For rational X(z) = N(z)/D(z), this is the most commonly used approach:

  1. Ensure X(z)/z is a proper fraction (degree of numerator < degree of denominator). If not, perform polynomial long division first.
  2. Factor the denominator to find poles
  3. Decompose X(z)/z into partial fractions
  4. Multiply back by z
  5. Look up each term in the standard pairs table
  6. Use the ROC to determine causal vs anti-causal for each term

Example: Distinct Poles

Find x[n] given X(z) = 2z² / [(z−½)(z−¼)], ROC: |z| > ½

X(z)/z = 2z / [(z−½)(z−¼)]

= A/(z−½) + B/(z−¼)

A = [2z/(z−¼)]z=½ = 2(½)/(½−¼) = 4

B = [2z/(z−½)]z=¼ = 2(¼)/(¼−½) = −2

X(z) = 4z/(z−½) − 2z/(z−¼)

x[n] = 4·(½)ⁿ·u[n] − 2·(¼)ⁿ·u[n]

Both terms are causal (u[n]) because the ROC is |z| > ½, which is the exterior of both poles.

Example: Repeated Poles

When the denominator has (z−a)ᵏ

For a pole of multiplicity k at z = a:

X(z)/z = ... + A₁/(z−a) + A₂/(z−a)² + ... + Aₖ/(z−a)ᵏ

Aₘ = (1/(k−m)!) · dk−m/dzk−m [(z−a)ᵏ · X(z)/z]z=a

The term Aₘ/(z−a)ᵐ corresponds to:

Z⁻¹{Aₘ · z/(z−a)ᵐ} = Aₘ · C(n,m−1) · aⁿ⁻ᵐ⁺¹ · u[n]

where C(n,m−1) is the binomial coefficient. For a double pole (k=2), the inverse includes both aⁿ and n·aⁿ terms.

Partial fractions are like disassembling a complex machine into standard components. Each fraction A·z/(z−a) is a "standard part" whose behavior (aⁿ) you already know. You identify the parts, look up their behavior in the catalog, and the total behavior is the sum. The ROC tells you which direction each part runs (forward in time or backward).

Method 2: Power Series Expansion

Direct long division

Since X(z) = Σ x[n]·z⁻ⁿ, the coefficients of z⁻ⁿ in the Laurent series ARE the signal values x[n]. To find them, perform polynomial long division of N(z) by D(z):

  • For causal signals: divide in descending powers of z (gives z⁻¹, z⁻², ...)
  • For anti-causal signals: divide in ascending powers of z (gives z, z², ...)

This gives you x[n] one sample at a time. It's useful when:

  • You only need the first few samples
  • The partial fraction approach is algebraically messy
  • X(z) is not rational

Example

X(z) = (1 + 2z⁻¹) / (1 − z⁻¹ + 0.5z⁻²)

Long division: 1 + 3z⁻¹ + 2.5z⁻² + 0.75z⁻³ + ...

→ x[0]=1, x[1]=3, x[2]=2.5, x[3]=0.75, ...

Method 3: Contour Integration (Residues)

When you need the closed form

Apply the residue theorem to the formal inverse:

x[n] = Σ Residues of [X(z)·zn−1] at poles inside C

For a simple pole at z = a:

Residue at z = a: limz→a (z−a) · X(z) · zn−1

When to use residues

This method is primarily used in theoretical derivations. In practice, partial fractions gives the same result with less machinery. But the residue approach is essential for:

  • Proving general results about inverse transforms
  • Non-rational X(z) where partial fractions don't apply
  • Computer algebra systems that evaluate contour integrals symbolically

The computational perspective: recursion IS the inverse transform

In digital implementations, we never actually "compute" an inverse Z-transform. Instead, we implement the difference equation directly — which IS the inverse transform being computed sample by sample in real time. When we write y[n] = x[n] + 0.5·y[n−1], we're computing the inverse of Y(z) = X(z)/(1 − 0.5z⁻¹) without ever mentioning z. The power series method is thus not just a mathematical technique — it's literally what your hardware does every clock cycle.

This perspective blurs the line between analysis and implementation. Formally, the inverse Z-transform and a recursive computation are distinct concepts that happen to produce the same outputs. The distinction matters for theoretical properties (convergence, causality) even if the numbers agree.
