Interactive Animations

Drag poles and zeros. Watch systems respond. Build intuition through play.

1. Pole-Zero Explorer

What you're seeing

The unit circle (gold ring) in the complex z-plane. Poles are marked × and zeros as ○. Click and drag them to move them around. The background colour shows |H(z)| — brighter means higher gain at that point in the z-plane.

So what?

The frequency response is what happens ON the unit circle. Drag a pole close to the circle and watch the gain spike at that angle (frequency). Drag a zero onto the circle and watch the gain drop to zero. This is how filter design works: sculpt the frequency response by positioning poles and zeros.

2. Frequency Response

What you're seeing

The magnitude response |H(ejω)| of a second-order system with conjugate poles at r·e±jθ. The horizontal axis is normalised frequency (0 to π). Adjust the pole radius (how close to the unit circle) and angle (which frequency resonates).

So what?

As radius approaches 1, the resonance peak gets narrower and taller — the system becomes more frequency-selective. At r = 1 exactly, the peak goes to infinity (instability). This is why highly resonant filters need high-precision coefficients: a tiny perturbation in r near the circle causes dramatic response changes.

3. Impulse Response Viewer

What you're seeing

The impulse response h[n] of a system with one conjugate pole pair. Each vertical bar is one sample. The envelope (dashed) shows the exponential decay rate rn. Change the angle to see oscillation frequency change; change the magnitude to see faster or slower decay.

So what?

The impulse response IS the system — it tells you everything about how it will react to any input (via convolution). A pole magnitude > 1 means the bars grow without bound (unstable). A pole at magnitude exactly 1 means they oscillate forever (marginally stable). Only magnitude < 1 gives a decaying, stable impulse response.

4. S-Plane to Z-Plane Mapping

What you're seeing

Left: the s-plane (continuous time). Right: the z-plane (discrete time). Points in the s-plane map to the z-plane via z = esT. The left half-plane maps inside the unit circle; the jω axis maps to the unit circle; horizontal lines map to circles; vertical lines map to radial rays.

So what?

This is why sampling matters. The mapping wraps the infinite jω axis around the finite unit circle — meaning continuous frequencies above π/T get aliased back. The sampling period T controls how tightly frequencies wrap. This is the geometric reason behind the Nyquist theorem.

5. Convolution in Action

What you're seeing

Top row: input signal x[n]. Middle row: filter impulse response h[n] (a simple moving average of adjustable length). Bottom row: output y[n] = x[n] * h[n] (convolution). Watch how the filter smooths the input by averaging neighbouring samples.

So what?

This is what Y(z) = X(z)·H(z) looks like in the time domain. The multiplication in z is THIS sliding/summing operation in time. A longer filter (more z⁻¹ terms in H(z)) means more smoothing but also more delay. The trade-off between smoothing and delay is fundamental to all filter design.
