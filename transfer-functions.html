From Difference Equations to H(z)

The standard LTI difference equation

Every linear time-invariant (LTI) discrete system can be described by a difference equation:

y[n] + a₁y[n−1] + ... + aₙy[n−N] = b₀x[n] + b₁x[n−1] + ... + bₘx[n−M]

Applying the Z-transform

Take the Z-transform of both sides, using the time-shift property (x[n−k] → z⁻ᵏX(z)):

Y(z)[1 + a₁z⁻¹ + ... + aₙz⁻ᴺ] = X(z)[b₀ + b₁z⁻¹ + ... + bₘz⁻ᴹ]

H(z) = Y(z)/X(z) = [b₀ + b₁z⁻¹ + ... + bₘz⁻ᴹ] / [1 + a₁z⁻¹ + ... + aₙz⁻ᴺ]

The transfer function H(z) completely characterises the system's input-output behaviour. Given any input X(z), the output is simply Y(z) = H(z)·X(z). This is the convolution theorem in action.

A transfer function is like a recipe card for a machine. You don't need to know the internal gears and springs — the card tells you: for any input ingredient (X(z)), here's the output product (Y(z) = H(z)·X(z)). Different machines (systems) have different recipe cards (transfer functions), but the format is universal.

Poles and Zeros

The DNA of a system

Writing H(z) in factored form:

H(z) = b₀ · [(z − z₁)(z − z₂)···(z − zₘ)] / [(z − p₁)(z − p₂)···(z − pₙ)]
  • Zeros (z₁, z₂, ..., zₘ): values where H(z) = 0. The system completely blocks input components at these complex frequencies.
  • Poles (p₁, p₂, ..., pₙ): values where H(z) → ∞. The system infinitely amplifies (resonates at) these complex frequencies.
  • Gain (b₀): overall scaling factor.

The pole-zero plot — poles marked as × and zeros as ○ on the z-plane — is the most compact and informative representation of a discrete system. From it you can read off stability, frequency response, impulse response shape, and more.

What Poles Tell You

Each pole contributes a mode to the impulse response

A pole at z = p contributes a term proportional to pⁿ in the impulse response h[n]:

  • Real pole, |p| < 1: Exponentially decaying mode. Closer to 0 → faster decay.
  • Real pole, |p| > 1: Exponentially growing mode — unstable!
  • Real pole, p > 0: Monotone (same-sign) decay/growth
  • Real pole, p < 0: Alternating-sign decay/growth
  • Complex conjugate pair: Oscillatory mode. Angle determines frequency, magnitude determines decay rate.
  • Pole at |p| = 1: Marginally stable — persistent oscillation (neither growing nor decaying)
Pole at z = r·ejθ:
— frequency of oscillation: ω = θ (radians/sample)
— decay rate: rⁿ (envelope)
— period: 2π/θ samples

What Zeros Tell You

Zeros shape the frequency response

While poles determine the natural modes (impulse response), zeros sculpt the frequency response:

  • Zero on the unit circle at angle θ: The system has zero gain at frequency ω = θ (a notch)
  • Zero inside unit circle: Minimum-phase — the system has the least possible phase shift for its magnitude response
  • Zero outside unit circle: Non-minimum-phase — extra phase shift occurs
  • Zero at z = −1: Blocks the Nyquist frequency (highest representable frequency)
  • Zero at z = 1: Blocks DC (removes the mean)
Poles are resonances — pluck the system there and it rings. Zeros are absorbers — signals at those frequencies vanish. A pole-zero plot is like an acoustic map of a room: poles mark where sounds amplify (resonant frequencies) and zeros mark where sounds cancel (dead spots). Move a pole closer to the unit circle and the resonance sharpens; put a zero on the circle and you get perfect silence at that frequency.

Frequency Response from H(z)

Evaluate on the unit circle

The frequency response H(ejω) — magnitude and phase as functions of frequency — is obtained by evaluating H(z) on the unit circle:

H(ejω) = H(z)|z=ejω

|H(ejω)| = magnitude response
∠H(ejω) = phase response

Geometric interpretation

At any frequency ω, the magnitude response is:

|H(ejω)| = |b₀| · [product of distances from ejω to zeros] / [product of distances from ejω to poles]

As ejω sweeps around the unit circle:

  • When it passes near a pole → denominator shrinks → gain peaks (resonance)
  • When it passes near a zero → numerator shrinks → gain dips (anti-resonance)
  • A pole on the circle → infinite gain (system is unstable/marginally stable at that frequency)
  • A zero on the circle → zero gain (perfect null at that frequency)

Cascade and Parallel Forms

Building complex systems from simple blocks

Because convolution becomes multiplication:

  • Cascade (series): H(z) = H₁(z) · H₂(z) · ... · Hₖ(z)
  • Parallel: H(z) = H₁(z) + H₂(z) + ... + Hₖ(z)
  • Feedback: H(z) = G(z) / [1 + G(z)·F(z)] (closed-loop)

This is why the Z-transform is indispensable for system design: complex systems decompose into simple, analysable building blocks whose individual transfer functions compose algebraically.

Transfer functions as a universal language

The transfer function concept transcends DSP. Economists model fiscal policy with H(z) relating stimulus to GDP response. Epidemiologists model infection dynamics with pole-zero systems. Neuroscientists describe neural circuits as cascaded transfer functions. Anywhere you have a linear system driven by discrete events, H(z) is the natural language. The fact that stability, frequency selectivity, and transient behaviour all read off the same diagram — the pole-zero plot — makes it one of the most information-dense representations in applied mathematics.

The "universality" claim applies strictly to LTI systems. Most real-world systems are nonlinear, and transfer functions only characterise their linearised approximations around operating points.
