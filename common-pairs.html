Common Pairs

The essential lookup table — signals you'll see again and again

δ[n] Unit impulse
u[n] Unit step
aⁿ Exponential
cos(ω₀n) Sinusoidal

Why memorise these?

In practice, you rarely compute Z-transforms from the defining sum. Instead, you decompose signals into combinations of these fundamental pairs and use linearity + properties to assemble the answer. Think of this table as your periodic table — the building blocks from which all discrete-time signals are constructed.

Deriving the Key Pairs

Unit Step: u[n] → z/(z−1)

From the geometric series

The unit step u[n] = 1 for n ≥ 0, so:

X(z) = Σn=0∞ 1 · z⁻ⁿ = Σn=0∞ (z⁻¹)ⁿ = 1/(1 − z⁻¹) = z/(z − 1)

This converges when |z⁻¹| < 1, i.e., |z| > 1. It's just the geometric series formula with ratio z⁻¹.

The unit step is like a tap that turns on at time 0 and never shuts off. Its Z-transform z/(z−1) has a pole at z = 1 — right on the unit circle. This makes intuitive sense: a signal that never decays (constant forever) sits on the boundary between stable and unstable. One nudge toward |z| < 1 and the sum diverges; stay at |z| > 1 and it converges.

Exponential: aⁿu[n] → z/(z−a)

The workhorse pair

Almost every Z-transform pair reduces to this one. Since aⁿu[n] is a geometric sequence:

X(z) = Σn=0∞ aⁿ · z⁻ⁿ = Σn=0∞ (a/z)ⁿ = 1/(1 − a/z) = z/(z − a)

Converges when |a/z| < 1, i.e., |z| > |a|. The pole is at z = a. If |a| < 1, the sequence decays and the pole is inside the unit circle. If |a| > 1, the sequence grows and the pole is outside — the system is unstable.

Sinusoidal: cos(ω₀n)u[n]

Euler's formula splits it into exponentials

Using cos(ω₀n) = ½[ejω₀n + e−jω₀n] and applying the exponential pair with a = e±jω₀:

Z{cos(ω₀n)·u[n]} = ½ · z/(z − ejω₀) + ½ · z/(z − e−jω₀)

= z(z − cos ω₀) / (z² − 2z cos ω₀ + 1)

The two poles are at z = e±jω₀ — a conjugate pair on the unit circle. This reflects the fact that an undamped sinusoid is marginally stable (neither growing nor decaying). Add a factor of aⁿ with |a| < 1, and the poles move inside the circle — a damped oscillation.

The Anti-Causal Pair

Same formula, different ROC, different signal

Here's a critical subtlety: X(z) = z/(z−a) corresponds to two different time-domain sequences depending on the ROC:

  • ROC: |z| > |a| → causal: x[n] = aⁿ · u[n]
  • ROC: |z| < |a| → anti-causal: x[n] = −aⁿ · u[−n−1]

The algebraic expression is identical — only the region of convergence distinguishes them. This is why the ROC is not optional metadata; it's essential for unique inversion.

The ROC is part of the Z-transform. Without it, X(z) is ambiguous.

Building Complex Signals

Combine pairs with properties

Any signal that appears in practice can be expressed as a combination of these pairs using the properties from the previous section:

  • Delayed exponential aⁿ⁻ᵏu[n−k]: time-shift the exponential pair → z⁻ᵏ · z/(z−a)
  • Exponential ramp n·aⁿu[n]: differentiate the exponential pair → az/(z−a)²
  • Sum of exponentials: partial fraction decomposition → sum of z/(z−aᵢ) terms

This is precisely how inverse Z-transforms work: factor X(z) into partial fractions, look up each term in this table.

The exponential pair as the atom of discrete systems

In continuous time, everything decomposes into complex exponentials est (the Laplace eigenfunctions). In discrete time, everything decomposes into geometric sequences aⁿ (the Z-transform eigenfunctions). The pair aⁿu[n] ↔ z/(z−a) is the atom: every rational Z-transform is a sum of these atoms (via partial fractions), every stable discrete system is a superposition of decaying geometrics, and every pole in H(z) contributes one such mode to the impulse response. Master this one pair and you've understood the entire machinery.

This "one pair rules them all" perspective is pedagogically powerful but technically limited to rational Z-transforms. Non-rational transforms (e.g., time-limited sequences with many terms, or transcendental functions of z) don't decompose this way.
